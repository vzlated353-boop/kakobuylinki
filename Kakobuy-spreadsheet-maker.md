## Getting Started: Planning Your Spreadsheet

Before you start building your spreadsheet, it's important to plan its structure and purpose. Consider the following questions:

**What's Your Primary Use Case?** Are you comparing products within a single category? Tracking prices across multiple categories? Building a wishlist for future purchases? Understanding your primary goal will help you design the right structure.

**What Data Points Matter Most?** Different shoppers prioritize different information. Do you care most about quality pass rates? Price? Order volume? Shipping time? Identify the data points that are most important to you and make them central to your spreadsheet.

**How Will You Organize Products?** Will you organize by category, by price range, by quality rating, or by some other criterion? The organization method you choose will affect how easy it is to navigate and use your spreadsheet.

**What Custom Criteria Do You Need?** Beyond the standard data from the main database, do you have specific requirements like color preferences, size constraints, or feature must-haves? Plan for custom fields that reflect your unique needs.

Once you've answered these questions, you can start designing your spreadsheet structure. A basic product research spreadsheet might include the following columns:

- Product Name
- Category
- Price
- Quality Pass Rate
- Order Volume
- Seller Name
- Seller Rating
- Direct Link
- Your Notes
- Priority Score (calculated)

You can add or remove columns based on your needs. For example, if you're researching electronics, you might add columns for technical specifications. If you're researching clothing, you might add columns for sizing and material information.

## Building Your Spreadsheet: Step-by-Step

Once you've planned your spreadsheet structure, follow these steps to build it:

**Step 1: Create the Basic Structure**
Open Google Sheets and create a new spreadsheet. Add column headers for each data point you've identified in your planning phase. Format the header row with bold text and a background color to make it stand out.

**Step 2: Import Data from the Main Database**
Access the main Kakobuy spreadsheet using the link at the top of this article. Identify products that match your criteria, then copy the relevant data (product name, category, price, quality pass rate, order volume, etc.) and paste it into your spreadsheet. You can import multiple products at once by selecting multiple rows in the main database.

**Step 3: Add Custom Fields**
Add columns for any custom criteria that matter to you. For example, if you're researching laptops, you might add columns for processor speed, RAM, and storage capacity. If you're researching clothing, you might add columns for size, color options, and material composition.

**Step 4: Create Formulas for Analysis**
Use formulas to automate calculations and analysis. For example, you can create a formula that calculates a value score based on quality pass rate, order volume, and price. The formula might look like this: `=(Quality_Pass_Rate * Order_Volume) / Price`. This gives you a single number that represents the overall value of each product, making comparisons easier.

**Step 5: Add Conditional Formatting**
Use conditional formatting to create visual indicators for key metrics. For example, you can set up rules that color-code cells based on quality pass rate: green for 90%+, yellow for 70-90%, and red for below 70%. This makes it easy to assess product quality at a glance.

**Step 6: Create Filters and Sorting Options**
Add filters to your header row to make it easy to sort and filter your data. This allows you to quickly find products that match specific criteria, like products under $50 with quality pass rates above 90%.

**Step 7: Test and Refine**
Once your spreadsheet is built, test it by importing a few products and using the filters, formulas, and formatting you've created. Make adjustments as needed to improve usability and accuracy.

## Advanced Techniques for Power Users

Once you've mastered the basics, you can use advanced techniques to make your spreadsheet even more powerful:

**Pivot Tables**: Use pivot tables to summarize and analyze your data. For example, you can create a pivot table that shows the average quality pass rate by category, or the total order volume by price range. This helps you identify trends and patterns in your research.

**Charts and Graphs**: Create visual representations of your data using charts and graphs. Bar charts can compare quality ratings across products, line charts can track price changes over time, and pie charts can show the distribution of products by category.

**Data Validation**: Use data validation to ensure that the data you enter meets specific criteria. For example, you can set up validation rules that require quality pass rates to be between 0 and 100, or that require prices to be positive numbers. This helps prevent data entry errors.

**Named Ranges**: Create named ranges for frequently used data sets. For example, you can create a named range for all products with quality pass rates above 90%, then use that range in formulas and charts. This makes your formulas more readable and easier to maintain.

**Macros and Scripts**: For advanced users, Google Sheets supports macros and scripts that can automate repetitive tasks. You can create a macro that imports new products from the main database, or a script that sends you email alerts when prices drop below a certain threshold.

**Integration with Other Tools**: Your spreadsheet can integrate with other tools and services. For example, you can use Google Forms to collect data from multiple sources, or use Google Apps Script to connect your spreadsheet to external APIs for additional data.

These advanced techniques allow you to create a truly powerful, customized research system that goes far beyond basic data entry and analysis. With the right approach, your spreadsheet can become an indispensable tool for making informed purchasing decisions.
